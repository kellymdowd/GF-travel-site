Gluten-Free Survival Guide

Easy
Key Phrases in Japanese
グルテンフリー Guruten Furī — Gluten free
小麦アレルギーがあります Komugi arerugī ga arimasu — I have a wheat allergy
醤油は使わないでください Shōyu wa tsukawanaide kudasai — No soy sauce please
セリアック病です Seriakku-byō desu — I have celiac disease
Safe Local Foods
  • Sashimi (plain, no soy sauce - or bring your own!)
  • Onigiri (my personal fav, but check for soy sauce-based fillings)
  • Yakitori with salt (shio), not tare sauce
  • Edamame
  • Mochi (check ingredients — most are GF)
Danger Foods
  • Regular soy sauce (koikuchi) — contains wheat
  • Tempura — wheat batter
  • Regular ramen — wheat noodles
  • Japanese curry — usually contains flour
  • Tonkatsu — breaded pork
  • Gyoza — wheat wrappers
Grocery Stores
Natural Lawson is your best bet for GF-labeled snacks and meals. Regular convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson) have some safe options like onigiri and edamame, but label reading is essential — most labels are in Japanese only.

Tokyo has an impressive number of dedicated GF restaurants — more than most cities worldwide. The language barrier is the biggest challenge, but a celiac dining card in Japanese goes a long way. Soy sauce and rice vinegar (which often contains barley malt) are the biggest hidden dangers.

Getting Around

JR Lines

The Yamanote Line loops around central Tokyo, connecting major hubs like Shibuya, Shinjuku, Tokyo Station, and Ueno. Your most-used line.

Runs 4:30am–1:00am

Metro & Toei

13 subway lines cover every corner of central Tokyo. Google Maps is essential for navigating transfers between Tokyo Metro and Toei lines.

¥170–¥320 per ride

Suica / Pasmo

IC cards that work on all trains, buses, and at convenience stores. Load at any station kiosk. Tap on, tap off — no tickets needed.

¥500 deposit, refundable

Walking

Tokyo neighborhoods are incredibly walkable. Most attractions within a district are within 10–20 minutes on foot. Station corridors can be long though.

Free, obviously
Pro Tip
Get a Suica card at Haneda or Narita airport immediately upon arrival. It works everywhere — trains, buses, vending machines, convenience stores. Download Google Maps with Japanese offline for subway navigation.

Landmarks & Must-See Spots

9 spots

Sensō-ji Temple

Historic

Tokyo's oldest temple, dating to 645 AD. The iconic Kaminarimon (Thunder Gate) and Nakamise shopping street leading to the main hall are unmissable.

Asakusa

Imperial Palace

Historic

The primary residence of the Emperor of Japan, surrounded by moats and beautiful gardens. The East Gardens are free to visit.

Chiyoda

Meiji Jingu

Historic

Serene Shinto shrine set in 170 acres of forest, dedicated to Emperor Meiji. Walk through the towering torii gate and see the consecrated sake barrels.

Harajuku

Gōtokuji Temple

Historic

The "lucky cat temple" — filled with thousands of maneki-neko (beckoning cat) figurines. A peaceful, less-touristy spot.

Setagaya

Shibuya Scramble Crossing

Iconic

The world's busiest pedestrian crossing. Watch from the Starbucks above or just dive in. Best at night when the neon lights up.

Shibuya

Hachikō Memorial Statue

Historic

The famous statue of the loyal Akita dog who waited for his owner at Shibuya Station every day. A beloved meeting spot.

Shibuya

Tsukiji Outer Market

Market

The original fish market's outer market still thrives with fresh seafood stalls, street food, and kitchen supply shops. Great for sashimi on the go.

Tsukiji

Tokyo Sky Tree

Historic

At 634m, Japan's tallest structure. The observation decks at 350m and 450m offer stunning city panoramas.

Sumida

Tokyo Tower

Historic

The 333m Eiffel-inspired tower, completed in 1958. Still iconic — especially beautiful lit up in orange at night.

Neighborhoods

Shibuya & Harajuku

Fashion · Nightlife · Youth Culture

The beating heart of Tokyo's youth culture. Shibuya has the famous scramble crossing and buzzing nightlife; Harajuku has Takeshita Street's quirky fashion and Meiji Shrine's peaceful forests. Several GF spots here including T's Kitchen and NachuRa.

Aoyama & Omotesando

Upscale · Cafés · Architecture

Tokyo's answer to the Champs-Élysées. Tree-lined Omotesando boulevard is flanked by luxury boutiques and stunning architecture. Great café culture — BREIZH Café and Rizlabo Kitchen are both here.

Asakusa

Traditional · Temples · Street Food

Old Tokyo at its finest. Sensō-ji Temple, traditional shopping streets, and the Sky Tree dominating the skyline. Shochikuen Cafe is a great GF find in this area — fully vegan and GF with an incredible rainbow cake.

Ginza & Marunouchi

Luxury · Business · Dining

Tokyo's upscale shopping and business district. Department stores, high-end dining, and Tokyo Station's beautiful redbrick facade. FANCL BROWN RICE MEALS and Soranoiro ramen are nearby.

Roppongi & Nishiazabu

Dining · Nightlife · Art

Known for its international dining scene and nightlife. Home to Sushi Korin (the GF omakase gem) and Kushiage Su. Mori Art Museum and Tokyo Tower are nearby.

Akasaka

Hotels · Government · Sushi

A mix of government buildings, hotels, and excellent restaurants. Itamae Sushi Hanare and Cafe Komaya are here. Close to the Imperial Palace gardens.

Shinjuku

Skyscrapers · Entertainment · Transit

Tokyo's busiest station area with towering skyscrapers, department stores, and the neon-lit Kabukichō entertainment district. A major transit hub for day trips.

Travel Tips

  • Get a Suica card at the airport immediately upon arrival — it works on all trains, buses, and at convenience stores.
  • Download Google Translate with the Japanese language pack for offline use. The camera translation feature is a lifesaver for reading menus and labels!
  • Carry a celiac dining card in Japanese at all times. Even at GF-friendly restaurants, staff appreciate being able to read your needs clearly.
  • Natural Lawson is your best friend for GF snacks — look for the green logo. Regular Lawson has fewer options.
  • Reservations are essential for popular GF restaurants, especially Gluten Free Kushiage Su — bookings open one month in advance.
  • Soy sauce is in almost everything Japanese. Always ask — many sauces and marinades contain wheat-based soy sauce even when you wouldn't expect it.
  • Sake is naturally gluten-free and widely available. Beer alternatives are harder to find — stick with sake, wine, or cider.
  • Many restaurants have picture menus or plastic food displays outside — use these to communicate even without Japanese language skills.
