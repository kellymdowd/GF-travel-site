Bratislava punches above its size for celiac travellers. EU allergen labeling is mandatory, and the city has multiple dedicated GF establishments. Traditional Slovak dishes are wheat-heavy, but a growing health food scene means safe options are plentiful.

Main Square

Historic

The heart of Old Town — Gothic, Neo-Baroque, and Romanesque architecture surrounding the Roland Fountain (1572). Home to the Old Town Hall and Bratislava City Museum. Fun to explore.

Blue Church

Historic

Art Nouveau gem built in 1913 — every surface painted in dreamy pastel blue. Such a cute, cool church! One of the most photographed buildings in Bratislava, about 10 minutes' walk from Main Square.

Bezručova 2, Staré Mesto

Historic

Iconic bronze statue of a cheerful sewer worker peeking out of a manhole. Legend says touching his head while making a silent wish will make it come true.

Neighborhoods

Staré MestoTourist hub
The pedestrian heart of Bratislava. Cobblestone streets, Baroque architecture, museums, cafes, and most sightseeing attractions. Home to Main Square, Blue Church, Čumil, and Michael's Gate. Where you'll spend the majority of your time.
PetržalkaPanelák district
Bratislava's largest borough, south of the Danube. Rows of socialist-era 'panelák' housing — fascinating for architecture buffs. Budget-friendly with reliable tram links to the centre. The Vienna train arrives at Petržalka station.
RužinovFamilies & shopping
Home to Bratislava Airport and the Avion shopping center. Near Zlaté Piesky lake for swimming and sports. Green spaces and the Aquapark make it popular with families.
Nové MestoModern & green
Modern district north of Old Town. Home to the Kamzík TV Tower observation deck and wooded hiking trails. More affordable than the centre with good transport links.
Karlova VesQuiet & residential
A former winemaking village west of Old Town with small-town charm. Green spaces, river access, and quick links to the centre. Popular with families and expats.

Getting Around

Walking
Old Town is compact and entirely pedestrian. Most attractions within 15–20 minutes of each other on foot.
Trams
Fastest and most reliable daytime transit. Runs approximately 05:00–23:30 across the city.
From €0.70/ride
Buses
Extensive network including night buses (prefix 'N'). Can get stuck in traffic during peak hours.
24hr pass €3.50
Train from Vienna
Under 1 hour city-to-city. Arrives at Petržalka station — take bus 80 to the centre.
~€12–16
Ticket tip
Buy tickets at yellow machines at stops or newspaper stands — you cannot buy from the driver. Validate immediately upon boarding. The Bratislava CARD includes free public transport, a guided walking tour, and discounts at 20+ attractions.

Travel Tips

  • Bratislava is an easy day trip from Vienna (~1 hour by train) but deserves at least 2 nights to explore properly — the city has more to offer than a quick stop allows.
  • The Bratislava CARD includes free public transport, a guided walking tour, and discounts at 20+ sights — great value if you're spending 2+ days in the city.
  • EU allergen labeling law means allergen number 1 on Slovak menus indicates gluten-containing cereals (wheat, rye, barley, oats) — look for this numbering system on printed menus and chalkboards.
  • Most traditional Slovak dishes use wheat flour by default (halušky, pirohy, knedlíky, schnitzel) — always verify before ordering and ask for GF versions specifically.
  • English is widely spoken in tourist areas and restaurants throughout Old Town — communication is generally straightforward.
What I'd do differently
  • Allocate a full day just for Bratislava rather than squeezing it into a multi-country trip — there's genuinely more here than expected.
  • Take the tram out to Sweet Street bakery — a dedicated 100% GF bakery is too rare to pass up, and the journey is short.
